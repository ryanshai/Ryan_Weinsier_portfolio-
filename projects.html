WorkFlow

- Download and Install PrucaSlicer
- Download nametag file and change text
- Register print job
- Print
- Execute color change
- Finish print

Process
For this project, I started by downloading and setting up PrusaSlicer. Once the software was configured, I downloaded the provided nametag .3MF file and customized the text with my name. I experimented briefly with font sizes before choosing one that I liked. I made sure to “slice” keep the color change feature active so the text would stand out once printed. After starting the print I watched the process until the printer signaled for the color change, and then I swapped the filament to get the two tone effect. Going over sections of the Prusa mini+ Handbook helped me better understand the filament loading process. The workflow gave me useful experience in preparing a model in PrusaSlicer and carrying out a successful print to jump off from.

Process
For my second print, I chose a castle-style torture test that featured tall towers and several steep overhangs. I deliberately sliced and printed it without supports to see how the Prusa MINI+ would handle challenging geometries. The vertical walls and towers came out fairly accurate, but the unsupported overhangs failed, bridges sagged and collapsed, leaving rough edges and incomplete features. This showed me that while the printer can reproduce simple and vertical shapes well, it struggles with designs that include large spans or unsupported angles unless supports are added. It also highlighted the design-side limitation: just because a 3D model looks good in CAD does not mean it is practical to print without modification.
Two Images Side by Side


In terms of file types, the STL I downloaded contained only the raw geometry of the castle, which meant I had to set up all of the slicing parameters myself in PrusaSlicer. A 3MF file, by contrast, can save not only the model but also the print settings, color changes, and other details, making it more useful for sharing ready-to-print projects.

Process
For my open-source assistive device, I printed a tool designed to help hold a book open with one hand. The device fits over the thumb and uses two angled wings to press down the pages, making it easier to keep the book open without needing both hands. This design is especially useful for people with limited mobility, or for anyone who wants to comfortably read while using their other hand for something else.

The print came out clean and functional on the first try. The documentation from Makers Making Change was clear and easy to follow, with well-prepared STL files and usage instructions. I found that the device worked as intended, though I noticed that the size and angle could vary depending on the thickness of the book, something that might be improved by offering multiple size options or making the design adjustable. Overall, this project demonstrated how 3D printing can be used to quickly and inexpensively create personalized assistive tools that improve accessibility in everyday life.

Project 2 – Remixing and Learning CAD Tools

Engineering 11a Project 2

Part 1
Square Image Example

Part 1: CAD Orientation – Heart Ring Trays

For my CAD orientation, I followed the Tinkercad tutorials through the Diamond Ring exercise to get familiar with the tools. For my own model, I designed two small heart-shaped ring trays. The trays had raised walls with a lower center so small items can stay inside. I then recreated the same tray in Fusion 360. While Tinkercad was faster for making the heart shape, Fusion gave me more precision in sketching and cutting the center area. Comparing them showed me how quick Tinkercad is for prototyping, while Fusion needs more setup but offers better dimensional control.

Square Image Example

Part 2: Tinkercad Remix – Key Hanger

For my first remix, I used a wall-mounted key hanger model. I changed it by removing some of the hooks, making the remaining hooks deeper so keys would not fall off, and adding extra hooks along the platform on top for more storage. In Prusa Slicer, I scaled it slightly, laid it flat, and printed it in PLA at 0.2mm layer height with 15% infill. Supports were needed for the hook overhangs, and in the future I might redesign the angles to avoid them. The result was a more practical version of the original model.

Part 3: Fusion Remix – Hydroponic Planter

For my Fusion remix, I chose a small hydroponic planter. I scaled the overall size down to make it more compact, but enlarged the water-pouring opening so that it would still be functional. Fusion made this easier because I could directly edit dimensions instead of only scaling the whole model. I sliced it in Prusa Slicer with the opening facing outward, printed at 0.2mm layer height and 20% infill, and added supports for the water channel. The final print was usable, though I realized that scaling down made the wall thickness borderline too thin — next time I would increase the thickness in Fusion for durability.

Reflection

This project showed me how the workflow from sketch → CAD → slicing → print affects the final result. Tinkercad was intuitive and quick for remixing, while Fusion offered more advanced precision. My designs all worked as intended, though each also revealed design limitations like thin walls or reliance on supports. If I wanted to optimize them further, I would adjust wall thicknesses, angles, and print settings to improve strength and efficiency. I learned how remixing existing designs can be a powerful way to adapt models to new uses and make them more functional for my needs.

Project 3 – Form and Fir for Electronics

Engineering 11a Project 3

Part 1
Square Image Example

Assembly and Soldering

I started with reading through the instruction manual and noting where the directions were clear or confusing. The diagrams were helpful, but some steps felt overly condensed, especially around component orientation.The parts came pre organized and I double-checked resistor values to prevent mistakes. While I was soldering I worked slowly and tried to use a strategy of soldering some of the smaller components first and moving to larger ones later. By the end, my radio worked, though I had to some redo components because I used too much solder.

Square Image Example

3D print Enclosure

After assembling the radio, I used digital calipers to take precise measurements of the circuit board, speaker, and knob. These measurements were very important for making sure my enclosure design would fit I sketched out the general shape and layout of the box and marked where the tuner speaker holes, and button acess would go. Having the physical kit in front of me made it easier to figure out how each component would fit together in three dimensions. I had to do this a couple of times and print thin fronts to make sure that all the knobs would slot into the enclosure properly.

Using Fusion I designed my first enclosure as a two-piece box that slots together with openings for everything that need to be acessable. My first iteration fit too tight because I added too much tolerance around the edges. After adjusting the measurements by about 0.5 mm and printing again, the second version fit much better. The only issue I had was that the design I had covering the speaker ripped out when it was done printing so I replaced it with a piece of lacey fabric which works just fine.

Lazer Cut Enclosure

For the laser-cut version, I recreated my enclosure as a finger-jointed box in Fusion and exported the parts as DXF files. We cut it and I sanded it a bit. Compared to 3D printing, laser cutting was faster but required more planning for assembly and fastening. The biggest challenge was aligning the finger joints so the electronics still fit snugly, when I tried to hot glue the pieces together I had to sand down a couple of the finger joints but after that they fit just fine. The first iteration fit well and felt sturdy and clean, with good openings for each component.

Reflection

This project helped me understand how different fabrication methods affect design decisions. 3D printing offers flexibility and complex geometry but requires attention to tolerance and print orientation. Laser cutting gave me a cleaner look and faster results but limits curves and depth. I learned how careful measurement, planning, and testing lead to better form and function.

Part 1

Problem identification, measurment and planning

I started by noticing that the thumb drives were constantly disorganized in the drawer. Because the drawer is shallow, most standard organizers didn’t fit. I wanted a solution that would keep each drive visible and accessible, while fitting the limited space.

I measured the internal dimensions of the drawer and then the dimensions of all my different USB and USB-C drives. The sizes varied quite a bit, so I decided to design a system that could hold them at a slight angle to save space vertically. I sketched a few layout ideas on paper and used those to figure out how many drives could fit per row and how to stagger them. Square Image Example

first design iteration

For the first prototype, I modeled the organizer with simple rectangular slots based on the average size of the drives. After printing/cutting the first version, I realized several problems: Some drives didn’t fit because their casings were thicker. The angle wasn’t steep enough, so the drives leaned out or fell over. The overall footprint didn’t fit well in the drawer. This helped me understand that the design needed more precise spacing and steeper angles.

Refinement and final iteration

I went back to the measurements and adjusted the slot width and angle. I also reoriented the layout so that the drives alternated directions to save more space. In this version, I focused on improving the ergonomics — making sure each drive could be easily grabbed without hitting the one next to it.

After a few digital tweaks, I fabricated the final version of the organizer. The drives fit snugly at a 30–40° angle, and the base matched the drawer’s shape perfectly. The result was a clean, organized layout that made it easy to see and access all my thumb drives.

Reflection

The project successfully solved my organization problem. Going through multiple iterations taught me how important precision and prototyping are in fabrication. Even small differences in measurements or angles had a big impact on the final usability. The final design is both practical and visually neat. The project file has been uploaded to the public domain and can be downloaded here: https://www.printables.com/model/1469140-angled-usb-and-usbc-thumb-drive-organizer
